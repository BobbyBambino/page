    •  Course Description

    From the course catalog:  Topics in Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometry, including some of the following: geometry from an
    axiomatic viewpoint, synthetic Euclidean geometry, transformation geometry and symmetry, affine and
    projective geometry, inversive geometry, spherical geometry, and hyperbolic geometry.

    Prerequisite

    Math 235 (Linear Algebra) and Math 236W (Discrete Math)

     

    Text

    David Singer, Geometry: Plane and Fancy, Springer, 1998.  You are expected to read the textbook.  Although many math books are not very readable, this book is "written to be read."  Although geometry is historically extremely rigorous, this text focuses much more on intuition and examples rather than detailed and technical definition.  Thus I hope you will find it readable and helpful.  Reading a math textbook is different than reading a history or biology book.  You should read with a notepad and paper by your side, taking notes, rewriting proofs and arguments in your own language, and coming up with your own examples to illuminate definitions.

    Homework

    Homework sets will be assigned as needed, roughly every two weeks.  You are encouraged to work on homework problems together, and you may turn in homework sets as group by writing each participant's name on the paper.  While it is no obstacle for productive group work to have both stronger and weaker group members, the active members in a group usually get tired pretty quickly of "freeloaders."  I encourage groups to omit names of "freeloaders" on the cover page.  Homework problems will be a balance of proofs, examples, counter-examples, and constructions.  Your homework will require scratch work where you are trying out different approaches to the problem.  You should work out all the details to the solution of a problem, have it all organized for content and flow, and then make your final write-up to be turned in.  The work you turn in should not jump all around on the paper.  It should be easy to read and neat.  You should copy the problem down on your paper with the solution below.  You are to write in complete sentences using proper grammar and punctuation.  The audience for which you are writing is another student in the class and the level of detail you include should reflect this.  Do not tear pages out of your notebook (unless it has perforated edges) and do NOT "stable-corner" your pages by folding them together.  The edges of your paper should be clean and your homework should be stapled together with a staple.
