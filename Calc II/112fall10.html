 Course Description

A continuation of MATH-111.Techniques of integration, applications of integration, improper integrals, polar coordinates, parametric equations, infinite sequences and series.

Prerequisite

High school record or placement test, or a grade of C- or better in MATH-108 or MATH-111.

 

Text

Jon Rogawski, Single Variable Calculus: Early Transcendentals, W.H. Freeman and Company, 2008. 

Grades

   100 pts (12 quizzes @ 10 pts each w/ lowest 2 scores dropped)
   300 pts (3 tests @ 100 pts each)
   100 pts (writing project)
   100 pts (homework presentations)
   100 pts (5 lab projects @ 20 pts each)
+ 150 pts (final exam)
   850 pts total

 

Quizzes

We will have 12 surprise quizzes, approximately one a week, over the semester.  Each surprise quiz will be worth 10 points and the lowest two will be dropped.  This is for either a poor performance on a surprise quiz or if you miss class on a day we are taking a surprise quiz for whatever reason.  The surprise quizzes will be only 1 problem taken directly from the homework assigned the previous week.  It could even be a problem that went over in class on Monday.  Of course, you will not know ahead of time when the surprise quizzes will be given (hence the adjective “surprise”).  The purpose of these quizzes is 1) to encourage you to come to class and 2) to give you feedback on your mathematical writing and understanding.

 
 

Tests

Three tests will be given in class on Wednesday September 22, Friday October 15, and Wednesday November 24.  Make-up tests will only be given under exceptional circumstances (to be documented), and only if you notify me prior to the exam date. Homework

Homework will be assigned on Wednesdays, Thursdays, and Fridays and will not be collected.  However most Mondays we will go over the previous week’s homework assignments (those assigned on W, Th, and F of the previous week) in class.  This will give you an opportunity both present and speak mathematics as well as field questions.  The insuring dialogue will hopefully make statistical concepts and thinking more clear to not only the presenter, but the class as a whole.  Before class, each student has the option of writing homework problems on the board which they would like to see solved.  If the problem you would like to see is already up, put a checkmark next to it.  If you would like to present a problem which is written on the board, write your initials next to it.  We will then present the problems in class, beginning with the problem with the most checkmarks and working our way down.  I will ask someone who has volunteered to present the problem.  If we finish all problems on the board, we will go over other assigned problems which anyone can be called upon to present.  Each student will present a minimum of three times.  Thus I will give those who have not had a chance to present preference when considered with those who have presented.  You will be graded out of 4 points determined as follows:  4 points- problem is done correctly with very minor errors, 3 points- problem is mostly correct with some errors, 2 points- major conceptual errors, 1 point- a train wreck, 0 points- you’re making up garbage on the spot and it’s obvious.  Your homework points will be weighted as I do not expect everyone to present 25 times over the semester.  If you are not in class and are selected to present a problem after all the problems on the board have been completed, you will earn 0/4 points unless you inform me ahead of time that you cannot make it to class or in the case of an emergency (sickness, death in the family, etc.)  You are encouraged to work on homework together.
