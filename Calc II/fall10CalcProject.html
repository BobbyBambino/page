Guidelines for Calculus Writing Project

 

You are to write a paper on a calculus topic of your choosing (one not directly covered in class) which discusses mathematics of your topic in both symbols (equations, formulas, etc.) and words.  The purpose of this paper for you to 1) discover and learn some mathematics totally on your own  2) get you to write about mathematics as opposed to simply doing plug-and-chug 3) provide an opportunity to explore some facet of math that may be pertinent to you in the future.  You must propose a topic to me no later than Thursday October 24.  The paper will be due on Thursday November 11.

How to choose a topic:  The easiest way to choose a topic is to choose to write about a section that we do not plan to cover in the book and which was not covered in Calculus I.  Try to choose a topic that may be relevant to your chosen major or field.  For example, section 8.2 concerns “Fluid Pressure and Force” which is of interest to many people in a variety of areas of science.  Of course, you are not limited to calculus topics which are only found in the text.  Perhaps you are a secondary education major and are interested in how students study calculus for the AP exam.  You can look up studies about that topic and write about that.  Or if you are interested in history, you may write about some aspect of the history of the development of the calculus. 

Grading:  I am breaking up the grading into two very broad categories each worth 50 points.  You will be graded on 1) your understanding of the math and 2) the coherence and writing in your paper.  All things “math related” fall into category 1.  Category 2 deals with small things like punctuation, sentence structure, capitalization, etc. but it also deals with global things like whether or not the paper is well written and readable, layout, presentation, etc.    

Requirements and Advice:

·        Cite your sources.  It is okay to take certain formulas or theorems as true without deriving them yourself in your paper, but if you do, you must cite a source which does go through the necessary math to arrive at your formula (or theorem or whatever).  If you make a factual claim, you need to have a source for where this claim can be found.  This is a necessary component in the broader world of scholarship, not just math, so it is important that you are careful about checking your facts for yourself as well as giving the reader a reliable reference. 

·        Use proper punctuation.  Mathematical equations and formulas can be parts of English sentences.  Hence proper punctuation is required.  If you wish to take the derivative of x2, then the following is appropriate:

Let f(x)=x2.  Then f’(x)=2x.

Notice that the second sentence reads “f prime of x is equal to two x” and that it is indeed an English sentence requiring a period at the end. 

·        Aim for roughly 3-5 typed, double-spaced pages.

·        If you are finding that typing math into Microsoft word is cumbersome, there is a program known as LaTeX which is designed for writing math (and other science) papers.  This is the standard software that all mathematicians use to write mathematical papers.  It is free to download, extremely easy to learn, and produces very beautiful documents.  If you are interested in using this program, please see me and I can help you get started.  In fact, those of you who are planning to take more math courses may wish to learn LaTeX now because you will find that it is much easier and neater to type your homework on than writing it by hand.       

·        Mathematical writing is not poetry, but that does not mean it must be completely technical, dry, monotonous, or devoid of all humanness.  You are writing to another human being in an attempt to explain a certain concept.  While formulas and symbols are often helpful, there is nothing wrong with including (in addition to necessary technical points and formulas) an explanation of what is going on or what you are trying to do in an informal or intuitive way.  Think about the definition of the derivative.  It is totally unclear what the limit formula is trying to do if you simply give it to someone as a definition, but if you explain where it comes from or why the definition is the way it is in words or pictures, the concept is much clearer to the reader.
