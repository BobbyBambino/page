Course Description

Graph theory is an exciting branch of mathematics containing deep and powerful theorems of wide applicability. One of the reasons for its popularity is its applicability in disciplines such as computer science, electrical engineering, and operations research as well as the more applied sciences. For example, graphs are indispensable tools in the design and analysis of communication networks. Apart from its wide applicability, graph theory is one of the hottest and fastest growing branches of pure mathematics due in large part to the ease in which new problems can be stated, understood, and solved by undergraduates. In this course, we introduce graph theory and many of its foundational results. Topics include: paths, cycles, trees, spanning trees, matchings, dominating sets, Mengers theorem, vertex coloring, chromatic number, planar graphs, and Kuratowskis Theorem.

Tests

There will be three in-class tests.  They will consist of short proofs, examples, counter examples, definitions, and statements of theorems. 

 

Quizzes

We will take the last 10-15 minutes of each Monday class for a small quiz which will cover the previous weeks material.  The main focus of the quizzes will be recalling definitions, statements of important theorems, examples, and counter examples.  The quizzes will not ask you to do any formal proofs. 

 

Homework

Homework sets will be assigned as needed, roughly once a week.  You are encouraged to discuss homework problems with at most 2 other people (excluding myself) per assignment. Groups of size 3 or less may turn in one homework assignment or you may turn in homework individually. Write down all the names of the other two people you worked on the homework with. Common write-ups or same odd mistakes on two papers from people in different groups will be investigated. Most people will not be able to look at the homework set and simply begin writing down solutions to turn in.  Proving things in mathematics requires a lot of scratch work where you are trying out different approaches to the problem.  You should work out all the details to the solution of a problem, have it all organized for content and flow, and then make your final write-up to be turned in.  The work you turn in should not jump all around on the paper.  It should be easy to read and neat.  You should copy the problem down on your paper with the solution below.  You are to write in complete sentences using proper grammar and punctuation.  The audience for which you are writing is another student in the class and the level of detail you include should reflect this.  Do not tear pages out of your notebook (unless it has perforated edges) and do NOT "staple-corner" your pages by folding them together.  The edges of your paper should be clean and your homework should be stapled together with a staple.

 

Late homework will not be accepted.  It must be turned in on the day it is due in class.
