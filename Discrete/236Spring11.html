    •  Course Description

    A course designed to bridge the gap between computation-oriented introductory courses and proof-oriented advanced courses. The language of contemporary mathematics, including the proper way to write mathematics, and the nature of mathematical reasoning. Extensive writing projects. Topics studied may include axiomatic systems, logic, set theory, functions, mathematical induction, graph theory and trees, permutations and combinations.

    Prerequisite

    Math-111 or permission from the instructor.  Most important is mathematical maturity.

     

    Text

    Carol Schumacher, Chapter Zero: Fundamental Notions of Abstract Mathematics (2nd Edition), Addison Wesley, 2000.  You are expected to read the textbook.  Although many math books are not very readable, this book is "written to be read."  Reading a math textbook is different than reading a history or biology book.  You should read with a notepad and paper by your side, taking notes, rewriting proofs and arguments in your own language, and coming up with your own examples to illuminate definitions.  There are many items marked "exercise" through out each chapter.  You should do as many of them as possible.  Items marked "problem" should also be attempted.  Some details of proofs in the text are left to the reader and you are expected to work through those details.  In addition, some of the boardwork and homwork problems will be on topics covered in the book, but that we didn't cover or only briefly touched on in class.  You may always ask about anything in the text that you do not understand in office hours.

    Boardwork

    We will devote each Monday class period to having you present boardwork problems.  You will receive the problem you are to present ahead of time.  The list of problems and those who are to present them can be found here (9:00-9:50) and here (10:00-10:50).  You are to explain in careful detail using correct logic how you arrived at a solution.  The class and I will ask questions about any details we don't understand or anything we think is missing.  I expect the class to participate, asking questions about anything they do not understand.  It is the job of the presenter to give a correct proof and explain it to the satisfaction of the class.  In these session we will work on mathematical thought, technique, and presentation.

    Homework

    This is a writing intensive course the homework is an integral part of the course.  Homework sets will be assigned as needed, roughly every two weeks.  You are encouraged to work on homework problems together but your final write-up must be your own.  Most people will not be able to look at the homework set and simply begin  writing down solutions to turn in.  Proving things in mathematics requires a lot of scratch work where you are trying out different approaches to the problem.  You should work out all the details to the solution of a problem, have it all organized for content and flow, and then make your final write-up to be turned in.  The work you turn in should not jump all around on the paper.  It should be easy to read and neat.  You should copy the problem down on your paper with the solution below.  You are to write in complete sentences using proper grammar and punctuation.  The audience for which you are writing is another student in the class and the level of detail you include should reflect this.  Do not tear pages out of your notebook (unless it has perforated edges) and do NOT "stable-corner" your pages by folding them together.  The edges of your paper should be clean and your homework should be stapled together with a staple.
